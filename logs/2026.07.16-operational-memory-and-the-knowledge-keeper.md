# AETHER Log #36 – From Documentation to Operational Memory

**Date:** July 17, 2026

Tonight felt like another turning point for AETHER, not because new infrastructure was deployed, but because the role of documentation became much clearer.

For months, BookStack has served as the central repository for documentation, runbooks, disaster recovery procedures, and infrastructure notes. Recently, however, a new pattern has emerged: BookStack is no longer just a place to *store* knowledge. It has become the authoritative source from which AETHER's AI can learn, teach, and preserve institutional memory.

## Mira Becomes the Knowledge Keeper

A conversation with Mira helped define her role within the architecture of AETHER.

Rather than acting as a general-purpose assistant, Mira is becoming the living knowledge layer of the homelab.

The architecture now feels naturally divided into distinct responsibilities:

* **Nora** – the body of AETHER; hosts infrastructure and services.
* **BookStack** – the library; the authoritative source of operational knowledge.
* **Gitea** – the historical record of source code and implementation.
* **Uptime Kuma** – the heartbeat; operational awareness.
* **Nexus** – the emerging common room where humans, automation, and AI coordinate work.
* **Mira** – the knowledge keeper who teaches, explains, connects information, and preserves operational context.

The goal is no longer simply to ask an AI factual questions.

The goal is for a new administrator to arrive knowing nothing about AETHER, have a conversation with Mira, and leave with a working understanding of:

* What exists.
* Why it exists.
* Where it runs.
* What depends on it.
* How it is maintained.
* How it is recovered.
* What has changed recently.
* Which risks are accepted.
* Which decisions were intentional.

This is no longer documentation alone.

It is operational memory.

## BookStack Ownership

An interesting realization tonight was that BookStack has effectively become Mira's domain.

Rather than manually maintaining documentation, I have shifted toward:

* building infrastructure,
* operating systems,
* performing assessments,
* and supplying context.

Mira, in turn, is responsible for transforming that operational work into clear, organized, and interconnected documentation.

Going forward, BookStack will continue to be updated through a collaborative workflow:

1. Complete infrastructure work.
2. Capture commands, outputs, screenshots, and reasoning.
3. Provide the information to Mira.
4. Allow Mira to update, reorganize, improve, and cross-reference BookStack.
5. Periodically refresh Mira's understanding of the entire documentation set so she continues to serve as an accurate operational guide.

This separation of responsibilities feels natural.

## AI as a Knowledge Interface

Another realization came from using the Sifted shipping platform at work.

Instead of manually searching enormous tables, filters, and reports, I simply asked the built-in AI to locate and structure exactly the information I needed.

It did.

That experience reinforced an idea I've been slowly building toward inside AETHER:

AI is most valuable when paired with well-organized information.

The documentation remains the source of truth.

The AI becomes the interface.

Instead of searching hundreds of pages, future conversations might simply be:

> "Mira, where is Nora's service inventory?"

or

> "How do I recover BookStack after a disk failure?"

Rather than searching manually, the knowledge keeper can synthesize information across multiple documents and explain it conversationally while still grounding every answer in BookStack.

That is exactly the direction AETHER is moving.

## Security Assessment #001

Earlier today, the first formal internal security assessment of AETHER was completed from the Kali Linux workstation (Reeba) against Nora.

This represented an important transition in the maturity of the homelab.

Up to this point, most effort had focused on building infrastructure.

The security assessment shifted focus toward validating, documenting, and securing that infrastructure.

The assessment included:

* Network reconnaissance
* Service discovery
* Operating system fingerprinting
* Service verification
* Docker inventory correlation
* Host-level verification
* Documentation planning

Commands included:

* `nmap -sn`
* `nmap -sV`
* `nmap -O`
* `nmap -A`
* `ss -tulpn`
* `docker ps`
* `docker compose ls`

Every externally visible service was successfully identified and traced back to either a Docker container or a host process.

Confirmed services included:

* SSH
* Nginx Proxy Manager
* Homepage
* Uptime Kuma
* Gitea (SSH and Web)
* Memos
* BookStack
* Cockpit
* RustDesk relay infrastructure
* QEMU VNC consoles
* Test Nginx container
* The long-forgotten 2048 Docker container

Perhaps the funniest discovery of the evening was remembering that the mysterious service on port 8080 was simply a Dockerized copy of the 2048 web game that I had completely forgotten I deployed months ago.

Ironically, this became one of the strongest arguments for maintaining a proper service registry.

Nothing suspicious was discovered.

Every exposed service ultimately had a legitimate explanation once correlated with the host configuration.

## Service Registry

One major action item emerged from the assessment.

BookStack will gain a permanent Service Registry documenting:

* every service,
* every exposed port,
* compose project,
* container name,
* authentication method,
* operational purpose,
* backup location,
* dependencies,
* ownership,
* and recovery procedures.

Future security assessments will compare against this registry to detect configuration drift over time.

## Looking Forward

AETHER continues evolving from a collection of servers into an operational environment.

The infrastructure is no longer the final product.

The documentation, history, assessments, and accumulated knowledge are becoming equally valuable.

The vision is becoming increasingly clear:

Build real systems.

Document them thoroughly.

Assess them regularly.

Preserve the reasoning behind every decision.

Then create a knowledge layer capable of teaching the entire environment back to anyone who needs to understand it.

Tonight felt like another important step toward that vision.
